The Route

What is the North Coast 500?

The North Coast 500 is Scotland's answer to Route 66 — a 500-mile circular driving route that loops around the dramatic northern coastline of Scotland, starting and ending in Inverness. Launched in 2015, it has since been named one of the world's greatest road trips by National Geographic, Lonely Planet, and dozens of international travel publications.

The route takes in everything that makes the Scottish Highlands extraordinary: ancient mountains, deserted white-sand beaches, dramatic sea cliffs, whisky distilleries, Highland castles, remote fishing villages and skies so clear on a dark night you can see the Milky Way with the naked eye.

And Invergordon? It sits right on the eastern NC500 corridor — just 17 miles north of Inverness — making it one of the best-positioned towns on the entire route for accommodation, supplies, fuel and day trips into the surrounding landscape.

Invergordon: Your NC500 Base Camp

Most NC500 travellers drive straight through Invergordon without stopping. That's their loss. The town has fuel stations, supermarkets, accommodation and restaurants — everything you need to restock before heading north into the more remote sections of the route. But more than that, it has its own extraordinary story to tell.

The Mural Trail alone is worth a stop. Add Glenmorangie and Dalmore distilleries within minutes of the High Street. Add the Cromarty Firth — one of Scotland's most important wildlife habitats, home to dolphins, seals and over 30,000 overwintering birds. Add Fyrish Monument on the hill above town. Add Invergordon Museum, which tells the story of Invergordon, the Firth and the people here — with their bright and varied history, it's a highly recommended visit. Plus the stunning Black Isle, just on the opposite side of the Cromarty Firth, filled with walks, cafes, restaurants, shops, areas of beauty, beaches, boat trips, dolphin and whale lookout points — a stunning place alone that could require you to spend several days just skimming the surface. A little more tricky for cruise ship passengers with no vehicle, but anyone doing the NC500 or staying locally in a hotel or B&B would likely enjoy all of these suggestions plus a whole host more depending on how long they are staying locally.

NC500 Highlights

Top Stops Near Invergordon

All of these are within a day-trip distance from Invergordon — use the town as your base and explore in every direction.

🥃
5 min · Alness

Dalmore Distillery

One of the world's great single malt distilleries, producing whisky since 1839. The visitor experience is exceptional — dark, aromatic and steeped in Highland history. Book ahead.

Whisky · Visitor Centre
🥃
20 min · Tain

Glenmorangie Distillery

Scotland's most popular single malt, made in the tallest stills in the country. The Tain distillery has a superb tour and shop. The nearby town of Tain is worth exploring too — it's Scotland's oldest Royal Burgh.

Whisky · Visitor Centre · Royal Burgh
🦅
20–25 min · Evanton

Fyrish Monument

A dramatic replica of the Gates of Negapatam, built on the summit above Evanton in 1782 to provide work for local men. A steep but rewarding hill walk with panoramic views across the Cromarty Firth. Visible from Invergordon High Street on a clear day.

Walking · Views · History
⛪
35 min · Dornoch

Dornoch

A beautiful small cathedral town on the east coast. Home to Royal Dornoch Golf Club — one of the world's top links courses — and a stunning 13th-century cathedral. Madonna famously chose Dornoch to baptise her son. Unspoiled, quiet and very Highland.

Cathedral · Golf · Beaches
🏰
45 min · Golspie

Dunrobin Castle

The most northerly of Scotland's great houses, and one of Britain's largest. Its fairytale French château exterior rises above immaculate formal gardens overlooking the sea. Home to the Earls and Dukes of Sutherland for centuries. Falconry displays in summer.

Castle · Gardens · History
🐬
5 min · Invergordon

Cromarty Firth Wildlife

One of Scotland's most important wildlife habitats — over 300 harbour seals, one of only two resident populations of bottlenose dolphins in the UK, and 30,000 overwintering birds. Dolphin-watching boat trips depart from nearby Chanonry Point on the Black Isle.

Wildlife · Dolphins · Seals
🏛️
20 min · Tain

Tain — Scotland's Oldest Royal Burgh

A historic market town with a beautifully preserved medieval heritage. The Tain Museum tells the town's remarkable story, and the Collegiate Church of St Duthac — a pilgrimage site of kings — dates from the 14th century. A genuine Highland town that hasn't been polished for tourists.

History · Heritage · Museum
🏖️
30 min · Black Isle

Chanonry Point

One of the best places in the UK to spot bottlenose dolphins from dry land. The Chanonry Point headland juts into the Moray Firth and the rising tide pushes fish — and dolphins — close to shore. Arrive around two hours before high tide and bring binoculars.

Wildlife · Dolphins · Viewpoint
⛰️
25 min · Strathpeffer

Ben Wyvis

The great flat-topped mountain that dominates the skyline above Invergordon — visible from the High Street on a clear day. A serious but rewarding Munro at 1,046 metres, Ben Wyvis is one of the most accessible big mountains in Scotland. Snow-capped from October to May.

Munro · Hiking · Views

Before You Drive

NC500 Practical Tips

⛽
Fuel Up in Invergordon Morrisons in Invergordon consistently has good fuel prices and is easy to get in and out of. Once you head north from Tain, petrol stations become increasingly sparse — and sometimes closed. Fill up here for reliability, not necessarily cost (prices further north aren't always more expensive, but availability can be genuinely unreliable in remote areas). Don't push it.
📅
Best Time to Visit May–September for long daylight hours and the best weather. July–August is peak season — book accommodation weeks ahead. May and September offer the best balance of good weather and smaller crowds. But don't dismiss winter. October–March brings a completely different NC500 — frost-dusted glens, snow-capped peaks, near-empty roads, and on clear nights the Northern Lights (Aurora Borealis) dancing above the Highlands. Winter driving demands respect though: pack extra warm layers and waterproofs, carry a car breakdown kit, download offline maps before you leave, and check road conditions before heading into the more remote sections. Phone signal disappears quickly once you're away from the towns.
🛣️
Single-Track Roads Much of the NC500 runs on single-track roads with passing places. Pull into passing places to let oncoming traffic through — never stop on the road itself. Take your time. The scenery is worth it.
🦟
Midges — Scotland's Unofficial National Pest Think a midge is just a tiny fly? Think again. These are flying teeth biting machines that arrive in clouds and find every exposed inch of skin. June–August is peak midge season, and they don't limit themselves to woodland — riverbanks, loch shores, boggy ground, campsites, basically anywhere that's damp, still and sheltered. They're worst at dawn and dusk, and on overcast days with no breeze. Arm yourself: Smidge repellent is far more effective than DEET on Scottish midges, and a proper midge head net is not embarrassing — it's essential if you're spending any meaningful time outside in the evening. You have been warned.
📡
Mobile Signal Mobile coverage is good in Invergordon and the main towns, but drops to zero in many remote areas of the NC500. Download offline maps before you leave — Google Maps, Maps.me, or OS Maps. Don't rely on real-time navigation in the far north.
🏕️
Wild Camping Scotland has a legal right to wild camp under the Land Reform Act. You can pitch almost anywhere — with courtesy and care for the environment. Leave no trace. Many NC500 travellers wild camp along the route; it's one of the great freedoms of the Scottish Highlands.
🌦️
Weather Highland weather is unpredictable at any time of year. Pack waterproof layers even in summer. The Highlands can deliver four seasons in one day — but clear weather here is genuinely breathtaking. The BBC Weather app gives the most accurate local forecasts.
🐑
Watch for Sheep Sheep roam freely on many Highland roads and have absolutely no road sense. Drive slowly through moorland sections, especially around dawn and dusk. Hitting a sheep is more common than people expect and genuinely dangerous at speed.
