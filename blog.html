30 April 2026 · NC500 & Scottish Highlands

The NC500: It's a World-Class Drive — But There's No Need to Race

They say the North Coast 500 is one of the world's great road trips. Five hundred miles of raw Highland coastline, mountain passes, ancient castles, remote beaches and single-track roads that seem to stretch all the way to the edge of the world. Visit Scotland have championed it, travel writers rave about it, and anyone who has driven it will tell you the same thing — it lives up to the hype. It really does.

But here's the thing. Far too many people race it. And when you race the NC500, you miss the best of it. The best of the Scottish Highlands isn't always at the signed viewpoints with the car parks. It's on the road that runs along the water instead of the one that cuts inland. It's the village that doesn't have a sign on the main road. It's the moment you pull over — not because the guidebook told you to — but because there are four sheep standing in the middle of the road giving you a look that says they were here first, and you realise you're not actually in a hurry, and this is exactly where you're supposed to be. This is Scotland. Slow down.

Invergordon: The NC500 Stop Most Drivers Miss
Sitting on the northern shore of the Cromarty Firth in the Scottish Highlands, looking south across the water to the Black Isle, Invergordon is one of those places that most NC500 drivers pass without stopping. It doesn't feature on the big tourist boards. It doesn't have a famous viewpoint with a postcard-perfect car park. What it has is something better — a real Highland town with a Mural Trail that stops visitors in their tracks, a world-famous whisky distillery, a local museum packed with history, a working port, and dolphins and seals out in the Cromarty Firth if you know where to look. It's also around 35 minutes from Inverness, just over an hour from Loch Ness, and surrounded by castles, distilleries and Highland scenery in every direction. This is the Scotland that doesn't get advertised — and it's often the best kind.

Getting Here — Two Routes, Both Worth Taking
If you're travelling clockwise and heading south past Tain, keep an eye out for the Glenmorangie Distillery on your left — those enormous copper stills are genuinely visible from the road, though at that point your eyes may well be drawn to the spectacular views across the Dornoch Firth instead. Continue south and look for the Nigg and Arabella roundabout. Not far after it, take the right turn for Kildary — Ken's Garage is your landmark. Turn left away from the garage, then left again to go under the bridge — you'll pass beneath the very stretch of the A9 you just left. After that, just follow the road and it brings you all the way into Invergordon. The water stays on your left the entire way, and the views keep changing as you move deeper into the Firth — pull in as you pass through the quiet villages of Barbaraville and Saltburn, with the hills of the Black Isle reflected in the water across from you. It adds a few minutes to your journey. Worth every one of them.

If you're travelling anti-clockwise — personally, my favourite direction to drive the NC500 — take the first junction signposted for Invergordon at Alness. You'll come off near Morrisons and Lidl, which is useful if you need to restock the car for the road ahead. Follow the road round, take a left at the mini roundabout, and you'll find yourself driving past the famous Dalmore Distillery — one of the world's most recognised single malt Scotch whisky brands — sitting right on the Cromarty Firth shoreline. From there the shore road winds down towards the water, the town of Invergordon visible ahead and the Sutors — the two great headlands guarding the mouth of the Firth — out beyond. On a clear day, that view alone is worth the detour.

What You'll Find When You Arrive
The Invergordon Mural Trail is one of the largest collections of outdoor murals in Scotland — dozens of large-scale artworks covering buildings across the town, each one telling a story of the Highlands, its people, its history and its character. Free, outdoors, and guaranteed to make you reach for your camera. The Dalmore Distillery, one of the most recognised single malt Scotch whisky brands in the world, sits right on the shoreline. The Invergordon Museum tells the story of a town that has witnessed a remarkable slice of Scottish history — from a major naval base in two World Wars to one of Europe's most significant deep-water cruise ports. And out on the Firth, dolphins and seals are regular visitors. Find a quiet spot on the shore and give it a few minutes.

The Practical Stuff — Because This Is What NC500 Drivers Actually Search For
Public toilets — yes, we have them. Fuel — available in town. Supermarkets — Co-op, Farmfoods and Morrisons are all here if you need to restock the snacks and supplies for the rest of the route. Takeaways, cafés and hot food — plenty of options for a proper meal or a coffee before heading back onto the road. Parking is easy too — whether you're arriving in a car or a motorhome, you'll find space without the stress. Invergordon is a working town, not just a tourist stop, and that means it has everything you actually need.

The NC500 is one of the great things to do in the Highlands of Scotland. Drive it properly. Take the shore roads. Stop in the towns that don't have the big signs. Pull over when the sheep tell you to. And when you see the sign for Invergordon — don't drive past. You'll be glad you didn't.

The Renaissance Calls In — First Drone Shots of the Season

Today was one of those days that reminds you exactly why living in Invergordon is something special. The Fred. Olsen Renaissance was in port — and for the first time this season, the weather, the schedule, and a charged drone all lined up at the same moment.

Getting airborne over the Cromarty Firth with a ship of that scale in the frame is a different thing entirely. The contrast between the quiet rooftops of the town, the cherry blossom in full bloom, and the Renaissance sitting at the quayside is the kind of image that tells the whole story of Invergordon without needing a caption.

More shots to follow as the season gets underway. The 2026 cruise schedule brings some big ships to the Firth this year — plenty of opportunities ahead.

April 2026 · Welcome

Welcome to Explore Invergordon

Invergordon sits on the shore of the Cromarty Firth in the Scottish Highlands — one of the deepest natural harbours in Europe, and for a few months every year, one of the busiest cruise ports in Scotland.

This blog is written by a local. The aim is simple: to share what makes this town worth exploring, beyond the obvious. The Mural Trail, the distilleries, the history, the food, the people. The things that don't make it onto the generic tourist leaflets.

If you've arrived by ship and you're wondering what to do with your day ashore — start here. If you're planning a visit by road — also start here. And if you already know Invergordon and want to keep up with what's happening in and around the town, this is the place to check back.

More posts coming regularly throughout the season.
